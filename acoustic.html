Reading the meter

The level is a relative index, not calibrated decibels. Phone microphones vary and apply their own gain, so use Acoustic to compare spots and to judge whether a space is quiet or noisy, not to certify an absolute sound level.
Automatic gain and noise suppression are turned off for the measurement, so the reading tracks real changes in the room rather than a normalized one. Hold the phone still and away from your hand so handling noise does not color the reading.
The band bars show where the energy sits. A strong low band usually means plant or HVAC, a strong mid band means voices or traffic, and a strong high band means hiss, fans, or electronics.

What the level tells you

Masking works against detection. A loud, steady sound floor covers the noise of a forced door, a dropped tool, or footsteps, so a noisy approach is easier to defeat unheard. Note loud zones near sensitive entries as a weakness.
Quiet works against privacy. In a quiet room, normal speech carries through walls, doors, and vents, so a quiet corridor outside a meeting room is an eavesdropping exposure. Note where conversations could be overheard.
Audibility matters for response. An audible alarm or a shouted challenge has to beat the local sound floor. Where ambient noise is high, the alarm or voice may not carry.

Encrypted Vault

Hold the sensitive material behind a passphrase. Observed credentials, client secrets, scope you would not want read off a lost phone: Cache keeps them encrypted at rest with AES-GCM and a key derived from your passphrase, so the data on the device is unreadable without it. The passphrase is never stored, and nothing can recover the vault if it is lost.

This device does not expose Web Crypto in a secure context. Host the kit over HTTPS or install it as an app, then reload. The vault needs it to encrypt.

Contents

How the vault protects data

The contents are encrypted with AES-GCM, an authenticated cipher, so tampering with the stored vault is detected and a modified vault will not decrypt. The key is derived from your passphrase with PBKDF2 over many iterations and a random salt, which slows guessing.
The passphrase is never written to the device. The key lives in memory only while the vault is unlocked, and locking clears it. Only the encrypted blob, its salt, and its initialization vector are stored, none of which reveal the contents.
Because there is no stored passphrase, there is no recovery. A lost passphrase means lost contents. Choose something strong that you will remember, and keep an independent record if the data matters.

What belongs here

Sensitive material that should not be readable off a lost or seized device: credentials you observed, client contacts and secrets, scope and rules of engagement you would not want exposed, and notes on findings that are sensitive before the debrief.
The encrypted vault travels with the engagement backup, still encrypted, so a backup never carries the contents in the clear. Keep the assessment findings themselves in their own modules so they reach the report, and reserve the vault for what genuinely needs to stay secret.

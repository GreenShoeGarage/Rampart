Using the map

The coverage is a rough geometric estimate, not a verified view. It assumes a clear line of sight to the reach you set, so it does not know about walls, pillars, shelving, or glare that block a real camera. Use it to find candidate blind spots, then confirm them at the monitor or by walking into the gap.
Set each camera honestly. A fixed camera sees a cone, so set its aim and field of view to match what it actually frames. A dome or PTZ can sweep, so model it as full coverage within reach, and note that a PTZ only watches one direction at a time.
Reach is relative to the plan, not a calibrated distance. Shorten it where light is poor or the lens is wide, since detail falls off with distance.

What to look for

Blind spots over entries and approaches are the priority. A gap above a door, a loading bay, or a perimeter path is an unwatched way in.
Single coverage with no overlap is fragile. Where one camera is the only eye on a critical point, an outage or an obstruction blinds it entirely.
Coverage that points the wrong way is a common finding, a camera aimed at a wall or down an empty corridor while the real approach sits outside its cone.
Record the confirmed gaps as findings here and capture the camera and the gap in Exhibit.

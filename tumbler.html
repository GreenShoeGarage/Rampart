Lock Anatomy & Inventory

A reference for how cylinders work and how they are graded, plus a place to inventory the locks you own or manage and the keys that operate them. Built for documentation and asset control, not entry.

The pin tumbler

A pin tumbler cylinder is a row of spring loaded pin stacks sitting across a rotating plug. Each stack has a lower key pin and an upper driver pin. With no key, the driver pins cross the gap between plug and housing and jam the plug in place.

The correct key lifts every stack so the split between key pin and driver pin lines up exactly with the shear line, the seam where the plug meets the housing. With all the splits at the seam, the plug turns. That alignment is the entire principle.

The diagram is conceptual. Tumbler documents and grades hardware. It does not teach defeat techniques.

Security pins, in brief

Higher grade cylinders replace plain driver pins with shaped ones such as spool, serrated, or mushroom pins. These shapes make a cylinder resist manipulation and raise the bar on covert entry, which is why they show up on better hardware. For assessment purposes the takeaway is simple: the presence of security pins and a high grade body is a sign of a serious lock, and their absence on a sensitive opening is a finding.

ANSI / BHMA grades

North American locksets are graded by ANSI/BHMA against cycle and strength testing. Grade is the fastest way to judge whether the hardware on an opening matches its risk.

A Grade 3 lockset on a sensitive exterior opening is a documentable mismatch between hardware and risk. Note it and recommend an uprated set.

Keyway and key control

Beyond the body, two things govern how controllable a system is over time. An open keyway uses blanks anyone can buy, so copies multiply quietly. A restricted or patented keyway limits who can cut a blank, which keeps the key population accountable. Strong key control is often worth more to a client than a stronger cylinder, because it governs the slow leak of copies rather than a single point of failure.
